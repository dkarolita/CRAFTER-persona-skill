# Persona Taxonomy Insights

These insights summarize the research basis behind the CRAFTER persona workflow.

## Research Basis

The persona taxonomy was developed from a curated corpus of 98 personas used in requirements-engineering-related tasks across 41 academic publications. The analysis identified common human factors, recurring presentation styles, and domain-specific persona needs.

The taxonomy was later evaluated with 20 software practitioners. Practitioners found the taxonomy useful as a summary of human traits, a starting point for persona creation, guidance for interview/workshop questions, a basis for persona templates, and a way to reduce unsupported assumptions.

## Internal And External Layers

CRAFTER personas should separate human information into two layers:

- **Internal layer**: general information about the represented individual. This includes background and human characteristics that help readers understand the person across contexts.
- **External layer**: context-specific information about the represented individual. This includes information shaped by the domain, project, environment, tools, social setting, organization, culture, policy, and usage situation.

The external layer should be customized by domain and project context. The same facet can mean different things in different domains. For example, technology interaction in education may describe how someone uses technology for learning, while technology interaction in physical health may describe how technology supports health maintenance.

## Minimum Human Characteristics

Every CRAFTER persona should include, at minimum:

- **Motivation**: why the person acts, accepts, avoids, or prioritizes something.
- **Goals**: what the person wants to achieve.
- **Pain points**: problems, pressures, frustrations, concerns, or obstacles the person wants solved or avoided.

These three characteristics help requirements engineers and designers understand behavior, identify needs, and reason about product or system improvements.

## Internal And External Versions Of The Minimum Characteristics

Motivation, goals, and pain points should be captured at both levels when possible:

- **Internal motivation/goals/pain points**: broader life, personal, emotional, capability, confidence, or value-based drivers and tensions.
- **External motivation/goals/pain points**: project-, task-, environment-, organization-, policy-, technology-, or domain-specific drivers and tensions.

Also explain the relationship between the internal and external versions. Good personas show how the environment affects personal motivations, how project goals relate to life goals, and how context-specific pain points amplify or reduce internal concerns.

## Human Facet Checklist

Use these facets as prompts, not mandatory fields. Select only those relevant to the domain and evidence.

Internal/general facets:

- Demographic information: name, age, gender when appropriate, tagline.
- Personal attributes: attitude, behavior, personality, preference, interests, hobbies.
- Physical well-being: health status, health challenges, body-related constraints.
- Mental well-being: emotional state, mental challenges, confidence, stress.

External/contextual facets:

- Personal story: activities, achievements, memorable incidents, life experience, values.
- Interaction with technology: technology use, literacy, adaptation, device ownership, wearables.
- Skill level: domain skill, health literacy, digital literacy, professional capability.
- Education: formal education, learning experience, training.
- Environmental-influenced characteristics: spoken language, local setting, accessibility context.
- Human values: life values, family traditions, religious beliefs, cultural expectations.
- Socio-economic status: financial situation, affordability, income pressure.
- Work status: occupation, role, work conditions, income source.
- Family environment: living arrangement, family structure, caregiving, parental or family intervention.
- Geographic location: current location, rural/urban setting, distance, mobility.
- Collaboration and communication style: social interaction, work experience, complaint behavior, communication needs.
- Culture: cultural suitability, cultural norms, local practices.

## Presentation Insights

Personas should be easy to consume. Practitioners preferred concise, straightforward, well-structured personas because they help readers find relevant information quickly.

However, excessive brevity can remove important context and invite incorrect assumptions. A strong CRAFTER persona should balance readability with enough narrative and evidence to preserve the intended interpretation.

Use the requested output format, but preserve these principles:

- Include a narrative view for human context.
- Include bullet or structured sections for quick scanning.
- Include assumptions and evidence notes to prevent overconfidence.
- Include internal/external taxonomy factors to show what was considered.
- Include motivation, goals, and pain points as non-optional core content.

## Quality Checks

Before finalizing a persona, check:

- Does it include motivation, goals, and pain points?
- Are these separated into internal and external forms when evidence allows?
- Does it explain relationships between internal and external factors?
- Is the external layer customized to the specific domain and project?
- Does the persona avoid unsupported sensitive claims?
- Is the output concise enough to consume but detailed enough to prevent misinterpretation?
- Are assumptions marked clearly?
